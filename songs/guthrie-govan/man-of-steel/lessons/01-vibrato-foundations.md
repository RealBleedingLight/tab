# Lesson 01: Vibrato Foundations
**Phase:** 1 — Foundation
**Prerequisites:** None
**Estimated time:** 20 minutes
**You'll need:** Guitar, metronome, tuner

## Goal
Produce consistent, even wrist vibrato on any fretted note — 2 oscillations per beat at 60bpm, with the ability to delay vibrato entry until beat 2.

## Warm-Up (5 min)
Place your fretting hand on the neck in a relaxed position around fret 12. Without picking any strings, practice rotating your wrist back and forth — like slowly turning a doorknob. Your fingers should stay in contact with the strings but the motion comes entirely from the wrist. Do this for 1 minute. Then pick any note on the B string and sustain it for 8 seconds with no vibrato at all — just a clean, steady tone. Repeat 4 times. This teaches you to hear the "baseline" pitch before you start modifying it.

## Concept: Wrist Vibrato
Wrist vibrato is the foundation of expressive lead guitar playing, and it is central to Guthrie Govan's sound throughout the Man of Steel solo. Here is how it works mechanically:

- **The motion comes from the wrist, not the fingers.** Your fretting finger stays planted on the string. The wrist rotates (like turning a doorknob or key), which pulls the string slightly and releases it.
- **The pitch bends DOWN slightly (flat) and returns to the fretted pitch.** It should never go sharp — that sounds out of tune. Think of it as: fretted pitch → slightly flat → back to pitch → slightly flat → back to pitch.
- **The thumb acts as a pivot point** on the back of the neck. The wrist rotates around this anchor.
- **Even vibrato means each oscillation is the same width (how far the pitch dips) and the same speed (how fast it cycles).** Uneven vibrato sounds nervous and amateurish. Guthrie's vibrato is famously controlled and singing.

## Steps

### Step 1: Clean Sustained Tone
Place your 3rd finger on the B string, fret 15. Pick the note with a downstroke and let it sustain. No vibrato at all.
- **Do this:** Pick and hold for 4 beats at 60bpm. Focus on a clean attack and even sustain.
- **Listen for:** A clear, bell-like tone with no buzzing or muting. The note should ring evenly from start to finish.
- **Repeat:** 5 times. If you hear buzz or the note dies early, check your finger placement (right behind the fret, firm pressure).

### Step 2: Slow Vibrato — 1 Cycle Per Beat
Keep your 3rd finger on B string fret 15. Now add vibrato at one full oscillation per beat (60bpm). One cycle = the pitch dips slightly flat and returns to the fretted pitch.
- **Do this:** Pick the note and immediately begin vibrato. Use the metronome — each click should align with one complete vibrato cycle. Sustain for 4 beats (4 cycles).
- **Listen for:** The pitch should waver gently and evenly. Each dip should be the same depth. If the note goes sharp at any point, you are pushing the string sideways rather than pulling it — reset your wrist motion.
- **Repeat:** 10 times. Focus on making each of the 4 cycles sound identical.

### Step 3: Faster Vibrato — 2 Cycles Per Beat
Same position: 3rd finger, B string, fret 15. Now double the speed to 2 oscillations per beat at 60bpm.
- **Do this:** Pick and sustain for 4 beats. You should complete 8 total vibrato cycles (2 per click). This is a common performance speed for vibrato.
- **Listen for:** Evenness is more important than speed. If it sounds choppy or uneven, slow back down to Step 2 tempo and gradually increase. Each oscillation should still be the same width and speed.
- **Repeat:** 10 times, or until you can do 3 in a row that sound even and controlled.

### Step 4: Vibrato on Different Strings
Move the same vibrato technique to other strings and positions.
- **Do this:** First, high E string fret 17 — same 3rd finger, same wrist motion. Do 4 beats of vibrato at 2 cycles/beat. Then move to G string fret 14. Same exercise.
- **Listen for:** The vibrato should feel and sound the same regardless of which string you are on. The G string requires slightly more wrist force because it is thicker — make sure you are still getting a clear pitch oscillation, not just a subtle wobble.
- **Repeat:** 5 times on each string. If one string feels significantly harder, spend extra time there.

### Step 5: Delayed Vibrato Entry
This is a signature Guthrie Govan technique: he often picks a note cleanly and lets it sit for a moment before adding vibrato. This creates an expressive "bloom" effect.
- **Do this:** Set your metronome to 60bpm. Pick B string fret 15 on beat 1. Hold it with NO vibrato through beat 1. On beat 2, begin vibrato at 2 cycles per beat. Sustain through beats 3 and 4 with vibrato.
- **Listen for:** A clean initial attack that sits still, then smoothly transitions into vibrato. The transition should not be abrupt — ease into it. There should be no "jolt" when the vibrato starts.
- **Repeat:** 10 times. Then try the same on high E string fret 17 and G string fret 14.

## Checkpoint
- [ ] Can you produce even vibrato at 2 cycles per beat (60bpm) on the B string fret 15 for 4 beats?
- [ ] Can you produce the same quality vibrato on the high E string (fret 17) and G string (fret 14)?
- [ ] Can you delay vibrato entry to beat 2, with a clean sustained note on beat 1 and a smooth transition?

**All checked?** → Move to Lesson 02
**Not yet?** → Repeat this lesson next session. Focus on whichever checkpoint isn't passing.
