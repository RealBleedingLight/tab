# Lesson 05: Outro — Bars 49-52
**Phase:** 1 — Foundation
**Prerequisites:** Lessons 01, 02
**Estimated time:** 20 minutes
**You'll need:** Guitar, metronome, tuner

## Goal
Play bars 49-52 of the Man of Steel solo cleanly at 50% tempo with emotionally expressive sustained notes, wide vibrato, and a sense of resolution and space.

## Warm-Up (5 min)
This lesson is about feel and tone, so warm up accordingly. Spend 2 minutes playing any single note on the B string (fret 13 or 15) with your widest, slowest vibrato — really let the note sing. Try to make the vibrato as wide as you can while keeping it even. Then spend 3 minutes sustaining notes for their full duration: pick a note, and let it ring for 8 beats at 60bpm without cutting it short. Practice patience — the outro requires you to sit with notes rather than rush past them.

## Concept: Space and Resolution
The outro (Section F) is the emotional payoff of the entire solo. After all the technical fireworks in the middle sections, Guthrie strips everything back to sustained notes, wide vibrato, and silence between phrases. This is harder than it sounds. The challenge is not physical but musical:

- **Space is part of the music.** The rests and gaps between notes are not empty — they are where the listener processes what they just heard. Do not rush to fill them.
- **Resolution means "coming home."** The solo is in D minor, and the outro phrases settle back toward D and its related notes (F, A). These notes should feel like exhaling after holding your breath.
- **Wide vibrato = emotion.** In this section, Guthrie uses his widest vibrato — the pitch wavers more than in the opening theme. This communicates intensity through a single note rather than through many notes.
- **Dynamics decay naturally.** Think of each phrase starting at a moderate volume and naturally fading, like a bell that rings and decays. Do not maintain constant volume.

## Steps

### Step 1: Learn the Notes — Bars 49-52
Open your tab (tab.txt Section F or PDF page 4). Read through bars 49-52.
- **Do this:** Play each note slowly, no metronome. There are fewer notes here than in Section A — the phrases are spacious. Identify each note and rest. Pay attention to note durations: many notes are held for 2 or more beats.
- **Listen for:** Clean tone. Since each note is exposed (not hidden in a fast run), any buzz, muting, or poor intonation will be very audible. Take extra care with clean fretting.
- **Repeat:** 5 times through until you can play the correct notes from memory.

### Step 2: Sustain Full Durations
Now play through bars 49-52 with a metronome at 40% tempo, focusing entirely on holding each note for its full written duration.
- **Do this:** Set the metronome and play through. On every sustained note, count the beats in your head and do not move to the next note early. On rests, actually rest — lift your finger or mute the string. Let the silence exist.
- **Listen for:** Notes that ring for their full duration without dying. If a note fades too quickly, check your fretting pressure and pick attack. Also listen for the rests — are you actually letting silence happen, or are you bleeding notes into the rests?
- **Repeat:** 3 times. Resist the urge to speed up or fill gaps.

### Step 3: Add Vibrato
Add vibrato to every sustained note (anything held for a beat or more).
- **Do this:** Use your widest vibrato here — wider than what you used in the opening theme. The wrist rotation should be more pronounced, creating a bigger pitch oscillation. Still use delayed entry: let the note speak cleanly for a moment before the vibrato begins.
- **Listen for:** Rich, expressive vibrato that makes each note sound like it is singing. The vibrato should feel generous and open, not tight or nervous. If you are used to narrow vibrato, consciously push for more width.
- **Repeat:** 5 times through bars 49-52 with vibrato. Experiment with different vibrato widths to find what sounds most emotional and appropriate.

### Step 4: Dynamics — The Exhale
Shape the dynamics of each phrase in bars 49-52.
- **Do this:** These bars come after the solo's climax (the intense middle sections). The emotional context is like exhaling after intense exertion. Start each phrase at a moderate volume (not loud) and let it decay naturally as the note sustains. Do not add a crescendo. Let each phrase gently fade.
- **Listen for:** A sense of release and calm. The volume should taper naturally. If you are picking too hard, the notes will sound aggressive — soften your pick attack. The feeling should be bittersweet and reflective.
- **Repeat:** 3 times through with dynamic shaping. Record yourself on your phone if possible and listen back — dynamics are hard to judge while playing.

### Step 5: Performance Pass
Put it all together at 50% tempo.
- **Do this:** Play bars 49-52 three times through at 50% tempo with: correct notes, full durations, wide expressive vibrato with delayed entry, and decaying dynamics. Between each pass, take a breath and reset — just as you would between takes in a recording session.
- **Listen for:** Music, not an exercise. Each pass should sound like a complete emotional statement. If something feels "off" but you cannot pinpoint it, try recording and listening back — sometimes issues with timing or vibrato are easier to hear from the outside.
- **Repeat:** Until you achieve 3 passes that feel musical and connected. Note: "feel" is subjective here, but you should sense that the passage sounds resolved and calm, not rushed or mechanical.

## Checkpoint
- [ ] Can you play bars 49-52 at 50% tempo with all notes sustained for their full written duration?
- [ ] Is your vibrato wide and even on sustained notes — wider than what you used in the opening theme?
- [ ] Does the section feel relaxed and resolved, like an exhale — not tense or rushed?
