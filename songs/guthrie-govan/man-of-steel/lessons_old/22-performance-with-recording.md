# Lesson 22: Performance with Recording
**Phase:** 5 — Performance
**Prerequisites:** Lesson 21
**Estimated time:** 30 minutes
**You'll need:** Guitar, metronome, tuner, recording of "Man of Steel," speed-changing app (Anytune, Amazing Slow Downer, or YouTube speed controls), a way to record yourself (phone is fine)

## Context for the AI Guide
You are guiding Leo (intermediate guitarist) through the final lesson in his "Man of Steel" solo learning plan. The solo is in D minor, standard tuning, approximately 56 bars long. Leo has completed all previous phases and can play the full solo at 50%+ tempo without stopping. This lesson shifts focus from technical accuracy to musical expression. Leo will play along with Guthrie Govan's actual recording, compare his own playing to the original, and develop his ear for the nuances that make this solo sing. This is the culmination of the entire learning process — the goal is musical, not just technical.

## Goal
Play along with the Man of Steel recording at 70%+ tempo with expression and dynamics that sound musical (not mechanical).

## Warm-Up (5 min)
**Full solo at comfortable tempo.** Set the metronome to whatever tempo you achieved in Lesson 21 (your max clean full-run tempo). Play the entire solo once through. This is both a warm-up and a confidence builder — you know this solo now.

## Steps

### Step 1: Play along with the recording at 70%
- **Do this:** Load the Man of Steel track into a speed-changing app (Anytune, Amazing Slow Downer, Transcribe+, or use YouTube's playback speed controls). Set it to 70% speed. Play along with Guthrie.
- **Listen for:** Don't worry about matching every single note perfectly. Instead, listen to Guthrie's PHRASING. Pay attention to:
  - **Where does he push ahead slightly?** Some phrases lean forward.
  - **Where does he breathe?** There are micro-pauses between phrases that give the solo its vocal quality.
  - **Where does he dig in?** Certain notes get extra attack and vibrato.
  - **Where does he back off?** Not everything is at full intensity.
- **Repeat:** 2-3 times. Each time, try to absorb more of his phrasing and less of your own habits.

### Step 2: Push the tempo — find your edge
- **Do this:** Increase to 75% speed. Play along. Then try 80%. Keep pushing until you find the tempo where you can mostly keep up but it's a stretch — some notes might get simplified or missed, but you're riding the wave of the music.
- **Listen for:** The point where you shift from "playing the notes" to "hanging on." That edge is where growth happens. Note that tempo.
- **Repeat:** One attempt at each tempo increment (75%, 80%, maybe 85%). Find your edge and note it.

### Step 3: Record yourself (solo, no backing track)
- **Do this:** Set your metronome to your best clean tempo (from Lesson 21). Record yourself playing the full solo from bar 1 to bar 56. Use your phone, a DAW, a looper — anything. This is your reference recording.
- **Listen for:** While playing, don't think about the recording. Just play. Perform it. Pretend this is the take that matters.
- **Repeat:** Once is enough. If you really fumbled, do one more take.

### Step 4: Compare your recording to Guthrie's
- **Do this:** Listen to your recording. Then listen to Guthrie's original (at 100% speed). Then listen to yours again. Compare honestly on these four dimensions:
  - **Vibrato:** Is yours as wide and controlled as his? Guthrie's vibrato is one of his signatures — wide, even, expressive. If yours is narrow or wobbly, that's priority work.
  - **Bends:** Are you hitting target pitches? Guthrie's bends land exactly where they should. Record a few bends in isolation and check with a tuner if needed.
  - **Dynamics:** Is the arc there? Does your version go from quiet to loud to quiet, or does it sit at one volume the whole time?
  - **Feel:** Does your version sing, or does it sound like someone reading notes off a page? The difference is in the micro-timing, the vibrato choices, the way certain notes are emphasized.
- **Repeat:** Listen to the comparison at least twice. Write down your honest assessment.

### Step 5: Focus on the biggest difference
- **Do this:** Pick the ONE element from Step 4 where your playing differs most from Guthrie's. Spend 10 minutes specifically working on that one thing:
  - If it's **vibrato**: Go back to specific phrases and practice wider, slower vibrato. Aim for the "vocal" quality.
  - If it's **bends**: Use a tuner. Play the target note, then bend up to it. Repeat until your ear locks in.
  - If it's **dynamics**: Play through the solo focusing ONLY on volume control. Exaggerate the dynamics — whisper quiet in A, scream in C/D, fade in F.
  - If it's **feel**: Slow way down (40%) and play each phrase like you're singing it. Hum the melody while you play. Find the vocal quality.
- **Repeat:** 10 minutes of focused work on this one element. Set a timer.

### Step 6: One final performance
- **Do this:** One last full run-through. Set the metronome to your best clean tempo. Or better yet — play along with the recording at whatever speed you can handle. This is your performance. Play it like it matters.
- **Listen for:** Everything you've worked on across all 22 lessons — clean technique, smooth transitions, dynamic arc, emotional expression. It won't be perfect. It doesn't need to be. It needs to be musical.
- **Repeat:** Once. This is your performance. Own it.

## What's Next
- **Push tempo:** Return to earlier lessons to push section tempos higher. The goal is full speed with the recording. Get there gradually — 5% at a time.
- **Record monthly:** Record yourself playing the full solo once a month. Compare recordings over time. You'll be amazed at the progress.
- **Experiment:** Try the solo with different tones, effects, or amp settings. How does it feel with more gain? Less? Clean?
- **Transfer skills:** The techniques you've developed here — legato, vibrato, bending, dynamic control, phrasing — apply to everything. Consider tackling another solo. Tell Claude which song you want to learn next.
- **Maintain:** Play through the solo at least once a week to keep it in your fingers. Muscle memory fades faster than you'd think.
