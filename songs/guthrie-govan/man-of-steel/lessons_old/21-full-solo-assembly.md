# Lesson 21: Full Solo Assembly
**Phase:** 5 — Performance
**Prerequisites:** Lesson 20
**Estimated time:** 35 minutes
**You'll need:** Guitar, metronome, tuner

## Context for the AI Guide
You are guiding Leo (intermediate guitarist) through a practice session on Guthrie Govan's "Man of Steel" solo. The solo is in D minor, standard tuning, approximately 56 bars long. Leo has completed Phases 1-3 (individual sections), Lesson 18 (A+F at 65%), Lesson 19 (outer shell at 55%), and Lesson 20 (C+D at 50%, full middle at 40%). This is the first time Leo will play the entire solo from bar 1 to bar 56 without skipping anything. The priority is CONTINUITY over perfection. A few imperfect notes at tempo are vastly better than stopping. In performance, the music doesn't wait. Encourage Leo to keep playing through mistakes. Track which specific bars need isolated work.

## Goal
Play the complete solo (bars 1-56) start to finish at 50% tempo with no stops.

## Warm-Up (5 min)
**Drill your weakest 4 bars.** In Lesson 20, you identified the 4 weakest consecutive bars. Start today by drilling those:
- Play them at 40% tempo, 3 times through.
- Push to 50%, 3 times through.
- If you don't remember which bars were weakest, play bars 23-26 (the C→D transition) as a default — it's almost always a trouble spot.

## Steps

### Step 1: Full solo at 40% — the first complete run
- **Do this:** Set the metronome to 40% of original tempo. Take a breath. Play the entire solo from bar 1 to bar 56. **Do not stop for mistakes.** If you flub a note, keep going. If you lose your place for a second, jump to the next phrase you know and keep going. Pretend there's an audience. The music does not stop.
- **Listen for:** Overall flow. Don't judge individual notes during this run — you're listening for the big picture. Does it feel like one piece of music or six disconnected sections?
- **After the run:** Immediately note (write down or tell Claude) which bars had the worst mistakes. Be specific — "bar 22" is useful, "the fast part" is not.
- **Repeat:** Once. Just one run for now.

### Step 2: Isolate and drill the 3 worst bars
- **Do this:** Take the 3 most problematic bars from your first run. Spend 2 minutes on each one:
  - Play the bar in isolation at 35-40% until it's clean.
  - Add the bar before and after (3-bar context) and play that 3-bar chunk 5 times.
  - If the problem is a specific technique (a bend, a position shift, a fast run), isolate just that moment and drill it.
- **Listen for:** The specific issue. Is it a wrong note? A timing problem? A position shift that's not automatic yet? A bend that's not hitting pitch? Diagnose, then fix.
- **Repeat:** 2 minutes per problem bar. Set a timer — don't get sucked into one bar for 10 minutes.

### Step 3: Full solo at 40% — second run
- **Do this:** Set the metronome to 40% again. Full solo, bar 1 to bar 56. Same rules — don't stop for anything.
- **Listen for:** Improvement in the bars you just drilled. Are they better? Even slightly? That's progress. Also notice: did any NEW problem bars emerge now that the old ones are improving?
- **Repeat:** Once.

### Step 4: Push to 45%
- **Do this:** Bump the metronome to 45%. Full solo, start to finish. This is a stretch — some sections (probably A, B, E, F) will feel comfortable at this tempo, while C and D might be rough. That's expected.
- **Listen for:** Where does the tempo first feel like too much? That's your current bottleneck. Note it.
- **Repeat:** Once. If it fell apart badly, try once more at 42-43% (split the difference).

### Step 5: Push to 50% (if 45% was clean)
- **Do this:** Only attempt this if 45% felt manageable with minor imperfections. Set metronome to 50%. Full solo.
- **Listen for:** The dynamic arc of the entire piece. Quiet/hopeful opening (A) → building energy (B) → explosive technical peak (C+D) → winding down (E) → peaceful resolution (F). Even at 50%, this arc should be present. If everything sounds the same volume and intensity, you need to think more about dynamics.
- **Repeat:** Once.

### Step 6: Reflect on continuity
- **Do this:** This is not a playing step — it's a thinking step. Consider: during your best run today, did the solo feel like one continuous piece of music? Or did it feel like six sections performed in sequence? The difference matters. In a performance, the audience hears one story, not six paragraphs.
- **Important:** A few imperfect notes at tempo are better than stopping. In performance, the music doesn't wait. If you're stopping to correct mistakes, you're still in practice mode. This lesson is about shifting into performance mode.

## Tips for the AI Guide
When guiding Leo through this session:
- **Encourage continuity.** If Leo says "I messed up bar 22 and stopped," redirect: "Great that you noticed bar 22. Next run, play through it even if it's imperfect. We'll drill bar 22 separately."
- **Track problem bars.** Keep a running list of bars Leo mentions as difficult. These become warm-up material for future sessions.
- **Note max tempo.** Record the highest tempo at which Leo completed a full run-through without stopping. This is the benchmark to beat next session.
- **Celebrate the milestone.** This is the first time playing the entire solo. That's significant regardless of tempo.
